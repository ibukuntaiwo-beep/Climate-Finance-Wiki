## Overview

When a company reports its greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, it does not simply count what comes out of its own chimneys. The internationally accepted framework, developed by the [[GHG Protocol]], divides emissions into three scopes that together capture the full climate footprint of an organisation's activities. Understanding the three scopes is essential for reading corporate climate disclosures, evaluating [[Net Zero vs Carbon Neutral|net zero]] commitments, and spotting where [[Greenwashing|greenwashing]] is most likely to hide.

Scope 1 covers direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the company. A factory burning natural gas, a company vehicle fleet running on diesel, a cement kiln, these are Scope 1. They are the most straightforward to measure and the most directly under management control.

Scope 2 covers indirect emissions from purchased energy, primarily electricity and heat. When a company buys electricity from the grid, it is effectively responsible for the emissions produced to generate it, even though those emissions happen at the power station, not on-site. Scope 2 accounting has created a major market incentive for corporate purchases of renewable electricity, through instruments like Power Purchase Agreements and renewable energy certificates.

Scope 3 covers all other indirect emissions across a company's entire value chain, upstream and downstream. This includes the emissions from producing the raw materials a company buys, the emissions from business travel, the emissions from the use of its products by customers, and the emissions at end-of-life disposal. For most companies, Scope 3 is the largest category by far, often representing 70–90% of total emissions footprint.

## Why Scope 3 Is the Hardest and Most Important

Scope 3 is hard to measure because it requires collecting data from hundreds or thousands of suppliers and customers, many of whom operate in jurisdictions with no mandatory disclosure. The estimates are therefore approximate, and methodologies vary, making comparison between companies unreliable.

It is also the most important. For a financial institution, Scope 3 includes the financed emissions, the emissions attributable to the loans and investments it makes. A bank's direct emissions (its offices and servers) are trivial compared to the carbon embedded in the companies it finances. This is why frameworks like the [[TCFD|TCFD]] and [[IFRS S1 and S2]] increasingly require financial institutions to report Scope 3, and why coalitions like [[GFANZ]] have made financed emissions a central accountability metric.

For a fossil fuel company, Scope 3 includes the emissions generated when customers burn its products, which is the vast majority of the company's total climate impact. This is what makes fossil fuel reserves potential [[Stranded Assets|stranded assets]] under ambitious climate scenarios: the Scope 3 liability attached to them is enormous.

## Scopes and Net Zero

A credible [[Net Zero vs Carbon Neutral|net zero]] commitment must address all three scopes, not just Scope 1 and 2. The [[Science Based Targets Initiative]] requires companies setting net zero targets to cover Scope 3 emissions where they are material, which, for most large companies, is almost always. A net zero claim that silently excludes Scope 3 is one of the most common forms of incomplete disclosure, and a significant [[Climate Litigation|litigation]] and regulatory risk.

The [[GHG Protocol]] is the dominant standard for how scopes are measured and reported, making it the foundation on which all other corporate climate accounting sits.
